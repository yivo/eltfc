English Language Tense Forms CheatsheetPresent Tense
Present Simple
permanent states, repeated actions, daily routines
He works at a hotel.
general truths and laws of nature
It rarely rains in the desert.
timetables, programmes [future meaning]
The plane to London takes off at 6pm.
sports commentaries, reviews and narration TODO
Hill kicks the ball and passes it to Dawson (sport commentary).
Laura Hunt acts suprbly in the fiml (review).
So, the prince tells her ... (narration).
adverbs of frequency [how often?]
100%always75%usually50%often
25%sometimes10%rarely/seldom0%never
time expressions
every day/week/month/year, on Mondays/Tuesdays/..., in the morning/afternoon/evening, at night/the weekend
Present Perfect
action which started in the past and continues up to the present [especially with state verbs]
They have been friends for twenty years. (They are still friends.)
recently finished action whose result is visible in the present
She has picked a lot of apples. (You see apples in the basket.)
action which happened at an unstated time in the past
[time – not important, fact – important]
He has broken his arm. (Time isn't mentioned.)
action which has happened within a specific time period, which is not over at the moment of speaking, and may happen again
He has received four messages this morning.
(Morning isn't over, action may happen again.)
He received four messages this morning.
(Morning is over, action may not happen again.)
action which happened before another past action [usage with past simple]
Yesterday, he had already prepared the drinks when his friends came to party. (He prepared drinks first. His friends came afterwards.)
time expressions
how long, for (duration), since (starting point), lately/recently
already, yet, just, always, ever, never, so far
Have you seen any good films recently?
We have already seen this film.
present perfect is often replaced with past simple [modern lang]
I have done/did the homework. Here it is.
no difference in meaning with verbs: feel, live, work, teach
He has worked/has been working here for two years.
used to announce a piece of news, with no details
I have just seen the new boss. I was having a coffee when he came in.
Present Simple Continuous
actions taking place now, at the moment of speaking
I'm drinking coffee now.
actions that are going on around now, but not at the actual moment
He is working hard these days. Right now he's reading newspaper.
planned actions to do in the future [time, place, 99% probability]
Tom is having job interview tomorrow.
changing or developing situations
More and more forests are disappearing because of fires.
expression of irritation at actions which happen too often
You're always forgetting to pay the bills.
time expressions
now, at the moment, these days, at present, tonight, nowadays, still
state verbs doesn't have continuous tenses
Present Perfect Continuous
action which started in the past and continues up to the present [emphasis on duration, especially with time expressions]
She has been picking vegetables for two hours. (Still picking them.)
action which started and finished in the past and lasted some time [result must be visible now]
He is dirty. He has been playing football. (He is no longer playing football. His clothes is dirty now.)
expression of anger, irritation or annoyance
Who has been reading my business paper?
time expressions
how long, for (duration), since (starting point), lately/recently
I have known Tom for five years.
He has been working here since 2010.
no difference in meaning with verbs: feel, live, work, teach
He has worked/has been working here for two years.
Past Simple
action which happened at a definite time in past
[time is stated, already known or implied]
They spent their holidays in Odessa last summer. They had a great time.
action which began and finished in the past
Ben was football player for ten years. (He is not longer a football player.)
used to talk about the lives of dead people, even if the time is not stated

actions which happened immediately one after the other in the past
First, he read the message. Then, he called his boss.
used for past habits or states which are now finished
People rode/used to ride a horse a lot those days.
